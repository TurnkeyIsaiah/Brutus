# Brief: need-to-think-about-it

## Query and intent

- Target query: how to handle "I need to think about it" sales objection
- Primary objection phrase: "we need to think about it"
- Search intent: informational with high commercial intent (account executives losing late-stage deals)
- Why this query deserves its own page: this is the most expensive stall in sales because it lands at the moment the rep is closest to closing. Most ranking pages give generic "uncover the real concern" advice. This page wins by naming the three things that "let me think about it" actually hides (price, authority, trust) and giving a diagnostic question for each.

## Required unique angle

- Required intro angle: "Let me think about it" is never about thinking. It is one of three things the prospect did not want to say to your face: the price is not going to work, the person you are talking to cannot say yes, or they do not yet trust you enough to commit. Your job in the next 60 seconds is to figure out which one without asking it directly.
- Required psychology angle: post-pitch decision fatigue plus social cost of saying no. The prospect at the end of a demo does not want to say yes (commitment) or no (conflict). "Let me think about it" releases both pressures and lets them exit. Once they exit, the deal cools.
- Required example scenario: end of a SaaS demo. Prospect has been engaged through the pitch. Rep walks through pricing. Prospect says "this looks great, I just need some time to think it over." Rep mishandles by agreeing and scheduling a vague follow-up. Rep corrects by running the diagnostic question that forces price/authority/trust to surface.
- Required "when to walk away" condition: the prospect names a specific stakeholder concern outside their control (a CFO sign-off pending unrelated quarter close, a board approval that will not happen for 90 days, a security review they have not started). That is a real timeline, not a stall.
- Required Brutus coaching moment: Brutus detects "polite stall" language at end of pitch (vague timing, no specific concern named) and prompts the rep to run the diagnostic question instead of accepting the stall. Specific Brutus prompt example: "Don't schedule a follow-up. Ask which of the three blockers is in the way."

## Required sections

The draft must include:

1. H1 that matches the query naturally (e.g., "How to handle 'I need to think about it' without losing the deal")
2. Unique intro, 120-180 words, naming the three blockers framework
3. 3-5 turn example dialog of an end-of-demo stall. Rep mishandles by accepting and scheduling. Rep corrects by running the diagnostic.
4. Psychology section anchored on decision fatigue and the social cost of saying no
5. 3-5 counter-frames as diagnostic questions, one per blocker:
   - Price: "If we set price aside for a second, would the rest of this be a yes?"
   - Authority: "Is the thinking just on your end, or are there others who weigh in?"
   - Trust: "What would you need to see to feel confident saying yes?"
   - Plus a meta-frame: when all three come back clean, ask directly what is making them hesitate.
6. Common mistakes section: scheduling a vague follow-up, sending more case studies, dropping the price preemptively, asking "is it the price?" first (signals you think it is)
7. When to walk away section
8. Brutus-specific section explaining how Brutus listens for the polite-stall pattern at end of pitch and surfaces the diagnostic prompt in time for the rep to use it
9. Related objections section linking to siblings
10. FAQ section with 2-3 unique questions (suggested: "What if they really do just need time?", "How long should the diagnostic take?", "Should I ever just accept the stall?")
