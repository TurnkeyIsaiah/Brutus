# Brief: no-budget

## Query and intent

- Target query: how to handle "no budget" sales objection
- Primary objection phrase: "we don't have budget"
- Search intent: informational with high commercial intent (B2B reps stuck in mid-funnel deals that go quiet on price)
- Why this query deserves its own page: most ranking pages treat "no budget" as a price problem and offer payment plans or discounts as the answer. That is wrong for B2B. In B2B, "no budget" is usually an authority problem in disguise. The contact saying it cannot move budget. This page wins by naming that and redirecting reps from discounting to qualifying.

## Differentiation

- Closest sibling pages: `too-expensive`, `bad-timing`, `need-to-think-about-it`
- What makes this page meaningfully different from those siblings:
  - vs. `too-expensive`: that is a value or anchoring problem. This is an authority problem.
  - vs. `bad-timing`: that is a priority problem. This is a wrong-person problem.
  - vs. `need-to-think-about-it`: that is a late-stage stall covering price, authority, or trust. This is specifically the authority slice, surfaced as a budget objection.

  - Do not include generic "follow up next quarter" advice. That belongs on `bad-timing`.

## Required unique angle

- Required intro angle: "We do not have budget" usually means "I cannot move budget." A real "no budget" answer comes from someone who controls the spend, who has looked at it, and who has decided no. If your contact has not done all three, you are not getting a budget objection. You are getting an authority signal. The rest of this page is about acting on the signal instead of arguing with the words.
- Required psychology angle: lower-level contact deflecting because saying yes is not theirs to say. "No budget" feels safer for the contact than admitting they cannot approve it. Reps then waste cycles trying to discount their way past a person who could not have said yes at any price.
- Required example scenario: discovery call with a department-level manager. The manager likes the product and is engaged. Rep brings up next steps. Manager says "honestly, this is not in our budget for the year." Rep mishandles by offering a discount or a smaller package. Rep corrects by redirecting from price to decision process.
- Required "when to walk away" condition: a documented company-wide spend freeze (announced layoffs, missed quarter, cost-out program) and no internal champion willing or able to bring it up to a higher signoff. That is a real no-budget. Add to nurture and move on.
- Required Brutus coaching moment: Brutus detects a price-objection pattern coming from a contact who has not been confirmed as a decision-maker, and prompts the rep to ask about decision process before talking about price. Specific Brutus prompt example: "Don't discount. Confirm who controls budget for this category."

## Required sections

The draft must include:

1. H1 that matches the query naturally (e.g., "How to handle the 'no budget' objection in B2B sales (without discounting)")
2. Unique intro, 120-180 words, naming authority as the real problem
3. 3-5 turn example dialog of a discovery call with a department manager. Rep mishandles by going to discount. Rep corrects by going to decision process.
4. Psychology section anchored on the lower-level contact deflection dynamic
5. 3-5 counter-frames focused on authority qualification:
   - Authority test: "Help me understand. Is budget for this set by you, or by someone above you?"
   - Process question: "When something like this does get funded, what does the path usually look like?"
   - Multi-thread: "Who else would care about this if we made it work?"
6. Common mistakes section: offering an immediate discount, splitting the deal smaller without changing the conversation, accepting "call us next quarter" without identifying the real decision-maker, multi-threading without permission and burning the original contact
7. When to walk away section
8. Brutus-specific section explaining how Brutus catches a price objection coming from an unconfirmed decision-maker and prompts the rep to qualify authority instead of discounting
9. Related objections section linking to siblings
10. FAQ section with 2-3 unique questions (suggested: "Is 'no budget' ever a real price problem in B2B?", "How do I qualify authority without sounding rude?", "When should I multi-thread vs. wait?")
