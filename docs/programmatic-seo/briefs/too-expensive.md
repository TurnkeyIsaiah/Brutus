# Brief: too-expensive

## Query and intent

- Target query: how to handle "too expensive" sales objection
- Primary objection phrase: "this is too expensive"
- Search intent: informational with high commercial intent (sales reps actively losing deals to price)
- Why this query deserves its own page: it is the highest-volume sales objection on the open web, but most ranking pages treat it as one problem when it is three. This page wins by naming the three flavors and giving a different response to each.

## Differentiation

- Closest sibling pages: `competitor-is-cheaper`, `no-budget`, `need-to-think-about-it`
- What makes this page meaningfully different from those siblings:
  - vs. `competitor-is-cheaper`: that page is about a known alternative. This page is about a vague "expensive" with no comparison anchor.
  - vs. `no-budget`: that page is about authority and procurement. This page is about value perception at a moment of price reveal.
  - vs. `need-to-think-about-it`: that page is about late-funnel stalls. This page is about a direct price reaction.
- What should not be repeated from other pages:
  - Do not lead with "ask compared to what." That is the same opener every other page on page 1 uses. Lead with the three-flavor diagnostic instead.
  - Do not include generic "highlight ROI" or "differentiate from competitors" advice. Those go on other pages.

## Required unique angle

- Required intro angle: "Too expensive" is almost never a price problem. It is one of three problems that share the same words: a real budget gap, a value misunderstanding, or an anchoring reflex. The rest of this page tells you how to diagnose which one in under 30 seconds.
- Required psychology angle: anchoring. The first dollar figure a buyer hears in a category becomes their internal reference point. "Too expensive" is often a reflex against that anchor, not against your actual price.
- Required example scenario: end of a SaaS demo. Rep states the annual number for the first time. Prospect's tone shifts. Prospect says "wow, that is too expensive." Rep has not yet asked a single question about how the prospect arrived at that judgment.
- Required "when to walk away" condition: the prospect can name a hard ceiling and a documented reason for it (board cap, procurement policy, fixed annual contract value) and your minimum is above it. That is a fit problem, not an objection.
- Required Brutus coaching moment: in real time, Brutus detects the rep about to defend the price (justify, list features, drop a discount) and prompts the rep instead to slow down and run the three-flavor diagnostic before saying anything else. Specific Brutus prompt example: "Don't defend. Ask which kind of expensive."

## Required sections

The draft must include:

1. H1 that matches the query naturally (e.g., "How to handle the 'too expensive' objection without dropping your price")
2. Unique intro, 120-180 words, leading with the three-flavor framing
3. 3-5 turn example dialog showing all three flavors getting confused on one call (rep mishandling), then the corrected version
4. Psychology section anchored on price anchoring and reference-point bias
5. 3-5 counter-frames, one per flavor:
   - Real budget gap: switch the conversation to scope, not price
   - Value misunderstanding: pause and re-anchor on the cost of the unsolved problem
   - Anchoring reflex: ask what they expected the price to be and why
6. Common mistakes section: defending the price, immediately offering a discount, listing more features, asking "compared to what" as a reflex (it is fine as a follow-up, not as the opener)
7. When to walk away section
8. Brutus-specific section explaining how Brutus catches the moment a rep starts defending price in voice and surfaces the three-flavor diagnostic prompt live on the call
9. Related objections section linking to siblings
10. FAQ section with 2-3 unique questions (suggested: "Is 'too expensive' ever a real price problem?", "Should I ever discount on this objection?", "How do I tell anchoring from a real budget gap?")
